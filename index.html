About

I am a PhD candidate in Economics at Arizona State University (expected 2027). My research spans macroeconomics, international economics, and development economics, with a focus on firms, trade, and labor markets in Mexico and North America.

My current work studies how tariffs and rules of origin reshape North American supply chains, where foreign firms choose to locate in Mexico and what that means for regional welfare, and how labor market power interacts with informality in the Mexican economy.

Before starting my PhD, I completed an M.S. in Economics & Computation at Duke University anc worked as an economist in the General Directorate of Economic Research at Banco de México.

Working Papers

Tariffs, Rules of Origin, and the Reorganization of North American Supply Chains

Working paper · Draft available on request

I develop a global sourcing model with heterogeneous firms that choose sourcing sets and USMCA compliance to maximize profits, and use it to evaluate how tariffs and rules of origin jointly shape compliance rates, supply chain structure, and Mexican welfare.

Foreign Firm Footprints: Entry, Agglomeration and Welfare in Mexico

Working paper · Draft available on request

Foreign firms sort into Mexican labor markets based on agglomeration economies, input costs, trade costs, and export market access. Estimated entry patterns feed counterfactuals on how U.S. tariff impositions would affect firm location, input markets, and welfare across Mexican regions.

Labor Market Power and Informality

with Octavio Vera · Working paper

Documents differences in wage markdowns across the formal and informal sectors in Mexico, and proposes a theory in which monopsonistic firms pass the implicit tax of formal hiring onto workers through lower wages.
